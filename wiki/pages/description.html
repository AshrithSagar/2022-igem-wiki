Introduction

Fish has continued to be a vital part of our diet since ancient times. The annual fish consumption has increased not in India but around the globe as well. This has led to the development of an extensive aquaculture industry, but unfortunately, due to excessive industrialisation procedures, over-intensive exploitation, and poorly managed aquaculture farms have resulted in the establishment of significant bacterial disease epidemics in aquaculture. These bacteria can be transmitted by the consumption of raw or undercooked fish which are infected, and can lead to severeserious symptoms in humans, including but not limited to vomiting, diarrhoea and abdominal pain. ToSo as to terminate this issue, it is important to target the infection cycle in its early stages to prevent the bacteria from infecting the fish.

The problem

One of the most prevalent bacterial infections affecting a variety of marine fish and shellfish is Vibriosis. It leads to significant economic loses and affects multiple sectors, thus hindering the development of the country. Several Vibrionaceae species have been linked to vibriosis in marine animals. According to a recent study, the most prevalent species affecting aquaculture farms are V. parahaemolyticus, V. alginolyticus, V. harveyi, V. owensii, and V. campbelli. The pathogenicity of these species is aided by a wide range of virulence factors, which allows them to infect a wide range of hosts.(1.https://sci-hub.hkvisa.net/10.1002/aah.10045)

In humans, Vibriosisvibriosis results in acute gastroenteritis after consuming raw, undercooked, or improperly handled marine food. Occasionally, cases of septicaemia, ear infections, or wound infections in people who already have medical issues are seen (2.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4263241/ ). In fishes, Vvibriosis exhibits symptoms like fatigue and necrosis of the skin and appendages, which causes malformations of the body, delayed development, liquefaction of internal organs, blindness, opacity of the muscles, and eventual death.(1.https://sci-hub.hkvisa.net/https://doi.org/10.1002/aah.10045).

Our bacteria of interest to tackle this infection in fishes is Vibrio parahaemolyticus. It is a gram-negativegram negative halophile which has been found in estuaries and maritime environmentsenvironment (2.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4263241/ ).V. parahaemolyticus Vp consists of a novel adhesion factor called Multivalent Adhesion Molecule 7 (MAM7)—a surface protein present on the bacteria. MAM7 (location) , which It is responsible for the initial host-pathogen adhesion and can trigger the up-regulation of additional adhesins and virulence factors that are unique to the pathogen and the host cell. MAM7 on the bacteria forms a tripartite complex with the protein, fibronectin and the ligand, phosphatidic acid. It has been extensively studied in V. parahaemolyticus and hence it deemed to be fit for this study (3.https://sci-hub.hkvisa.net/10.1074/jbc.M111.291377 )

Current solutions

Currently, there are multiple solutions to this infection;,however, but antibiotics still till date remain to be the most popular strategy for treating and preventing this V. parahaemolyticus infection in fish. Antibiotics are frequently used with fish diets or baths to treat bacterial illnesses in real life. Antibiotic overuse has led to the emergence of multidrug resistance in severala number of dangerous bacteria, including Vibrios (4.https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27052711/). Our solution

Our solution involves the synthesis of a novel antimicrobial peptide that can bind to the protein MAM7 on the bacteria, thus preventing the infection.
